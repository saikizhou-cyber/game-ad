# Creative Strategy and Format Routing

## Start from product truth

Extract the smallest interaction that creates a visible state change. A strong game ad usually sells one of these experiences:

- order from chaos;
- rescue from danger;
- mastery after obvious failure;
- collection and completion;
- transformation and upgrade;
- risky choice and consequence;
- speed, precision, or power;
- social comparison, status, or identity;
- curiosity about what is hidden or unlocked.

Write the product truth as:

`When the player does [input], [state] changes into [payoff].`

If the sentence is unclear, do not add narrative yet.

## Build the attention contract

The hook promises a specific kind of reward. The rest of the ad must pay that promise.

| Hook family | Viewer question | Required payoff |
|---|---|---|
| Imminent mistake | “Will they stop it?” | prevent, fail, or reveal consequence |
| One move from order | “Can this be fixed?” | clean, complete organization |
| Hidden reveal | “What is under/inside it?” | readable reveal tied to play |
| Impossible challenge | “Is there a solution?” | demonstrated solution or compelling fail |
| Character crisis | “What happens to them?” | game action changes the outcome |
| Sensory promise | “How satisfying will this get?” | escalating visual/audio release |
| Contradiction | “Why is this happening?” | fast explanation, then product proof |
| Choice | “Which would I pick?” | consequence of a clear decision |

Do not use a hook only because it is visually loud. Use it if it creates a retention question the product can answer.

## Creative form router

| Conditions | Primary form | Why | Main risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core loop is immediately legible and satisfying | Pure gameplay/ASMR | Fastest proof, low narrative cost | monotony; weak first-frame context |
| Failure is dramatic and reversible | Fail-to-win challenge | Strong tension and comments | overlong failure; fake logic |
| Audience trusts peer behavior | UGC/creator reaction | Native platform grammar | weak gameplay visibility |
| Character stakes can causally trigger play | Live-action or AI short drama + product | Emotional retention | disconnected “bait then game” |
| Product art/world is the selling point | 2D/3D showcase | High aesthetic control | low interaction proof |
| Exact UI and repeated revisions matter | AE gameplay/motion design | Deterministic and scalable | sterile feel if sound/cadence are weak |
| Impossible location/character is central | AI narrative/visual metaphor | High novelty per asset | continuity and text defects |
| Several forms have complementary strengths | Mixed media | Can maximize hook and proof | handoff discontinuity |

## Concept scorecard

Score 1-10 and write one-sentence evidence for each score:

1. Hook strength in the first three seconds.
2. Immediate readability on a phone.
3. Retention question.
4. Product/gameplay truth.
5. Sensory payoff.
6. Production confidence under current deadline and budget.
7. Continuity across production methods.
8. Localization and policy safety.
9. Ability to produce variants.
10. Distinctiveness from references and current campaign.

Recommended selection logic:

- eliminate any concept scoring below 6 on product truth or production confidence;
- prioritize the highest combined hook + readability + payoff score;
- keep a backup with a different hook family, not merely a different color grade;
- for medium/high risk, prototype the first three seconds and the method-transition shot before full production.

## First-three-second test

Pause on frames at roughly 0.0s, 0.5s, 1.5s, and 3.0s. A viewer should infer:

- what object/person matters;
- what is wrong or desirable;
- what action is imminent;
- why the next second might resolve it.

If all meaning comes from subtitle copy, redesign the visual hook.

## Creative risk tiers

- **Low**: gameplay-forward, existing assets, deterministic edit, proven hook grammar.
- **Medium**: new UI, AI inserts, character narrative, complex transition, or substantial fake setup with true gameplay payoff.
- **High**: full generative continuity, hard-to-control body interaction, exact in-generation UI/text, deceptive product behavior, or single-shot production dependency.

For high-risk concepts, create a fallback path before generation starts.
